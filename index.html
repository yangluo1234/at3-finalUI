Overview

Sound Constellation is an interactive audio UI where users connect stars to build a melody and then replay the path as a constellation line. The concept blends a night sky metaphor with a playable synthesiser. The project responds to the “No text characters” creative response, and the UI is built on the provided Tone.js audio system.

Guiding Values and How They Shaped The UI

Clarity means the interface communicates through shape and motion instead of text. The stars act as the only main controls. The play and clear buttons sit below the field and are easy to find.

Playfulness comes from the scattered layout and the glowing look. Users can click any star, hear a tone, and draw a line that forms a small melody.

Immersion is created by sound and light working together. Each star gives a short pulse, and the notes have small natural variations. This makes the night sky feel alive.

Feedback, Information Design, Mapping, And Learnability

The UI gives immediate feedback. Each click plays a note at once, and the star flashes brighter with a short scale pop, while the canvas draws a line from the last star to the new one. The background hue shifts slightly when Play runs, and small timing and velocity randomness make repeats feel alive. The reverb icon grows and changes colour as the slider moves, so the effect feels visible as well as audible. The twinkling animation signal interactivity without text, which fits the no-text characters prompt. The Clear button wipes lines and memory, giving a clean reset when users want to start a new constellation.

The information design follows a clear hierarchy. The sky is the main stage in the centre. The controls sit below and, the footer stays at the bottom. Stars and lines live in the sky layer, while play, reset, and reverb stay in the controls layer to keep things tidy. High contrast guides focus, with bright stars on a dark background and yellow accents on buttons. Because I follow the no-text characters prompt, meaning is carried by icons, motion, and sound

The mapping uses a scattered night-sky layout rather than a grid. Each star is a direct control, and users can click in any order. The system draws a line after each click, so the visual path mirrors the musical sequence. This freedom mapping feels like sketching a constellation and encourages open exploration.

Learnability relies on familiar iconography and immediate feedback. The play triangle, reset arrows, and water-drop reverb icon act as quite signposts, so most users already know what they do. After one click, the system confirms the choice with sound, a brief star flash, and a line drawing, which teaches the interaction without instructions. Because controls are few and consistent, most users succeed on first use and build confidence quickly.

Significant Change From Prototype To Final UI

First, I replaced the 4*4 grid with a scattered night sky layout because the grid looked like a tool, not a constellation. The free positions let users draw any path, and the screen reads more like a sky. This change also aligns better with the no text characters prompt because the idea is carried by spatial composition rather than labels.

Second, I added a twinkle animation to each star, so the page feels alive before the first click. The motion is slow and small to avoid distraction. In quick tests with classmates, most people noticed the stars and understood they were interactive without me telling them. A few people said the twinkle could be slightly stronger on small screens, so I increased the mid keyframe scale and opacity.

Third,I increased contrast in the controls by moving to yellow accents on a deep blue black background. The goal was to improve legibility and make the play and reset icons readable at a glance. I checked contrast with simple eyedrop tests, and the buttons now stand out while still feeling part of the night theme.

These changes improved immersion and clarity, but they also introduced trade-offs. The scattered layout is less regular, so I had to pre-place the stars with percentages to keep balance across screens. If I had more time, I would add a small onboarding hint that fades after the first tap, and a toggle to adjust twinkle strength on very bright displays.

Production Timeline In 9 Weeks

Week 1

I set an early goal to fix the concept and build a small test. I first tried a no text idea with geometric shapes. It worked but felt flat. User actions did not feel meaningful. This helped me realise that the metaphor matters as much as the sound.

Week2 - Week 3

After research, I shifted to a constellation idea. The star metaphor made immediate sense. It supported free drawing and listening. I kept the scope small so I could deliver a working path from click to sound.

I built the prototype with a 4*4 grid. My goal was clarity. The grid looked like a canvas so users could understand clicking and connecting quickly. It worked, but the mood felt too mechanical and less like a night sky. This tension between clarity and character became my main design question.

Week 4 - Week 6

I rebuilt the interaction around scattered stars. Each click plays a note and adds a point to a path so drawing and sound match. I kept the logic simple and stable. I also reworked the visual hierarchy. Stars and lines stay in the sky layer while the play, reset and reverb controls sit below. I began increasing contrast so controls read better on a dark background.

Week 7 - Week 8

I focused on polish and feedback. I added a gentile twinkle using CSS animation and a short flash on click. These cues make the stars feel alive without adding text. I also changed button styling to a darker base with yellow accents to improve contrast and keep the theme of night and starlight. Much of this time was research and small trials, since several techniques were outside class material.

Final week

I tested my project again and again, cleaned up small inconsistencies and added code comments so the mapping and drawing logic are clear.

Looking back, most time went to concept and interaction decisions rather than pure coding. The grid prototype taught me that clarity can reduce character. The final scattered sky keeps learnability but adds atmosphere and freedom. I learned to fix one core flow early, ship it, then improve it with focused changes based on feedback and testing.

The Aspect I’m Most Proud Of

I am most proud of the idea and how it is presented in the final UI. At the start, I only had a simple plan to use different shapes to trigger different sounds. It was very basic and did not feel meaningful. Through research and brainstorming, I found a clearer concept. Turning the interface into a night sky where users draw their own constellation made the interaction feel open and poetic, not just functional.

I am also proud that I could realise this idea with self learning. I taught myself the parts I was missing, like using CSS animation for a gentle twinkle and a short flash on press, and mapping user taps to lines so the drawn path matches the melody. I learned to keep the interface mostly text-free and rely on icons, motion and sound to guide the user. This balance of concept and implementation shows growth in both design thinking and technical skills.

Finally, I value how the UI feels when you use it. The scattered stars invite exploration, the audio has small randomness for a natural touch, and the controls are clear at a glance. This combination came from feedback, testing and iteration, and it reflects the kind of work I want to keep doing.

The Aspect I'm Most Challenging

The most challenging part was the audio side. I started with almost no music theory and I did not know what delay, filter, reverb or even notes really meant. Even reading explanations in my first language felt confusing because these terms were new to me and I was not very interested in music before. For this project I had to push myself to learn the basics. I studied simple scales and note names, and I read how common effects shape the sound.

This learning changed how I designed the UI. I kept the controls small in number so users do not face the same confusion I had. I chose one clear slider for reverb because it gives an immediate sense of space, and I left delay and filter as internal settings rather than complex panels. I also added tiny randomness to note timing and velocity to make the sequence feel more natural. I tested by ear, compared outcomes, and adjusted until the feedback sounded right.

In the end, I learned the fundamentals that I actually needed. I can now describe what reverb does, how a filter changes brightness, and how note choice affects mood. This was harder for me than coding or CSS, but it made the final interface more thoughtful and easier to use.

Next steps

My next step is to make the constellation feel richer without adding heavy UI. I plan to offer two or three preset skies that load different note sets so people can explore different moods. I also want to add a save and share option so a user can copy a link and replay their drawing.

I will continue to improve feedback. The twinkle will have small variation in speed and brightness, so the sky feels more alive. The play path will glow while it is read so timing is easy to follow.

On the technical side I will refine performance on mobile. I may also run a short user test with my friends to measure learnability. I will ask them to draw a shape, play it, clear it, and explain what they think the slider does. Their feedback will guide the next round of polish.
